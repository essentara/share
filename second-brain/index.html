01 The idea

Your AI is a genius with amnesia.

Here's the thing nobody tells you when you start using AI seriously.

The model is brilliant — but every new chat, it has total amnesia. It doesn't remember you, your projects, how you like things done, or what you decided last week. So you re-explain yourself. Forever. Every conversation starts from zero.

A second brain is just the memory you give it once — a folder of plain text it reads before it helps you. That's the whole trick. Everything else on this page is detail.

Once the AI can read who you are, how you work, and what's happening in your life, its help stops being generic. It stops sounding like a press release and starts sounding like a partner who's been paying attention. And because it's just text files, it compounds: every time you correct it or teach it something, that lands in the folder and it's smarter the next time — not just for one answer, but for good.

↓ Let's open the hood. It's simpler than it looks.

02 Anatomy

Six parts. One folder.

The whole system is a folder on your computer. Inside, each file has one job — and that's the secret to why it stays clear instead of becoming a junk drawer.

stable
Who you areSOUL.md
Your story, your values, how you're wired. The AI reads this and its help starts fitting your actual life instead of a generic one.
stable
The rulesCLAUDE.md
How you want it to behave. Your boundaries, your do's and never-do's, how it should talk to you. Read at the start of every session.
changes weekly
Right nowworking-context.md
Where you are this week, what's loud, what's paused. Keeps it current so you never have to re-explain your situation from scratch.
grows itself
What it's learnedmemory/
Preferences and corrections, saved so it never makes the same mistake twice. You barely touch this — it fills itself as you work.
live
The worldyour apps
Calendar, email, tasks, notes — connected so it works with real data, not a stale copy you have to keep updating by hand.
across all of it
Who does the workthe agents
You + your AI in a live session. And — optionally — an always-on agent that runs on its own. Both read the exact same brain. (More on that below.)

The golden rule that keeps it from turning into a mess: each file has one job, and doesn't do another file's job. Rules go in the rules file. Today's chaos goes in the "right now" file. That discipline is 80% of why it works.

03 The three documents that start everything

If you do nothing else, do these three.

You don't need the whole system on day one. You need three text files. Here's what each one is, why it matters, and a template you can take home right now.

SOUL.mdwho you are

What it is: The doc that tells your AI who it's actually working with — your story, your values, how you make decisions, what you'll never compromise on.

Why it matters: This is the difference between generic advice and advice that fits. Mine opens with six lines — roughly "musician → went viral singing people to sleep → burned out → walked away → now building something that integrates all of it." Suddenly every suggestion my AI makes is shaped to the real arc of my life, not a stranger's.

CLAUDE.mdthe rules

      
What it is: The operating manual. SOUL says who you are; this says how to behave — your rules, your boundaries, how it should route work and talk to you.

      
Why it matters: One line in mine says "never schedule anything 9am–noon." Now nothing — not a meeting suggestion, not my own agent — ever touches my creative mornings. When the AI gets something wrong, I add a rule here and it's fixed for good. It's a manual you write once and edit forever.

working-context.mdright now

      
What it is: The whiteboard. The one file that changes often — where you are this week, what's loud, what you've set down.

      
Why it matters: Mine right now basically says "heads-down before a trip, creative projects are paused, these three threads are loud." So my AI stops pitching me shiny new ideas and helps me land the plane. Update it weekly; it's the difference between an assistant that knows your situation and one you brief from scratch every time.

    04 What it actually does for me
    
The point isn't the folder. It's the time you get back.

    
A few things this system does for me every week. None of it is magic — it's just an AI that finally has context. (Examples sanitized; the real ones have my clients' names in them.)

  

  

    

      

        🌅
        
The morning briefing

        
Before I'm awake, one note is waiting: today's calendar, the three emails that actually need me, what's due, what I said I'd do. I read one thing instead of opening five apps.

      

      

        🗂️
        
Brain-dump → organized

        
I ramble thoughts into a notes app all week. Once a week it sorts the chaos into projects, tasks, and ideas — nothing lost, nothing I have to file by hand.

      

      

        💬
        
Inbox triage, in my voice

        
It reads my unread messages and drafts replies that actually sound like me. It never sends without me — I skim, tweak, approve. An hour of pinging becomes ten minutes.

      

      

        🧠
        
Ask my own brain

        
Months of notes, bookmarks, and voice memos get compiled into a little knowledge base I can ask. "What do I know about X?" → a cited answer pulled from my own life.

      

      

        🗓️
        
Plan my week

        
It reads my task list, estimates how long things take, and lays them onto my calendar — around my protected hours, never through them.

      

      

        🎙️
        
Voice memo → notes

        
I talk for five minutes walking the dog. It turns the monologue into structured notes and routes the to-dos to the right place. Thinking out loud, finally captured.

      

    

    
Notice the pattern: it's not doing my work for me. It's clearing the gravel so I can do the work only I can do.

The pieces, if you ever want to build one

      
Not a how-to with commands — just the map, so the idea isn't a black box. This is a weekend project for the technically curious, not a day-one move.

      

        
1
A VPS
A small cloud computer (think ~$5–10/month) that's always on. "VPS" just means a rented computer that never sleeps.

        
2
The agent runtime
The always-on agent software running on that computer — the thing that actually does the work when you're gone.

        
3
A schedule
Timed triggers ("cron jobs") that kick off tasks on their own — the 6am briefing, the weekly clean-up, the nightly digest.

        
4
A channel to reach you
A messaging app (I use Telegram) so it can send you what it made and ping you when it needs a human.

        
5
Shared files
The same second-brain folder synced to both your laptop and the server (via GitHub), so both agents read and write the one brain.

      

      
Don't start here. Get real value from the folder + your three docs first. Add the always-on layer when you find yourself wishing the work just… happened without you. That's the signal you're ready.

    

  







  

    06 Start yours
    
Twenty minutes to a brain of your own.

    
You can have the core of this working before your coffee gets cold. Here's the on-ramp.

  

  

    

      
Make the folder
Create a folder somewhere you'll keep it — ~/SecondBrain/. That's it. It's just a folder.

      
Drop in the three docs
Copy in SOUL.md, CLAUDE.md, and working-context.md (below) and fill the [brackets]. Rough and honest beats polished and vague.

      
Point your AI at it
In Claude (Cowork or Claude Code), open the folder as your project — it reads CLAUDE.md every session. In any chat AI, paste SOUL + CLAUDE into custom instructions or a Project.

      
Ask it something real
"Given what you know about me, what should I focus on this week?" Watch it use your context. That's the moment it clicks.

      
Let it remember
When it learns something true about you, have it write a line to MEMORY.md. Next session, it remembers. That's the compounding part.
