The one idea

01Your AI is a genius with amnesia

Every time you open Claude or ChatGPT, you re-explain a bunch of stuff about yourself. Not because it's dumb — it's brilliant. It just has no permanent, updating context on your life. So every conversation starts from zero.

A second brain fixes exactly that. It's not a notes app with AI bolted on. It's a folder of plain-text files — written in normal English — that your AI reads before it does anything. Who you are, how you work, what you're up to, what you've decided. So you're not talking to a stranger. You're talking to something that's known you for years.

And here's the part that hooked me: it compounds. Every session feeds back into the folder, so the thing gets smarter the more you use it. Genius with amnesia → genius who remembers everything. It's a self-improving loop, and the engine is almost embarrassingly simple.

Six parts, one folder. That's literally all it is.And it lives on YOUR computer — not uploaded to OpenAI, not to Anthropic. You own it. Switch models, switch tools, the brain stays yours.
The vocabulary

02The words you need

Most of the confusion is just unfamiliar words. Learn these ten and 80% of the fog lifts. None of it is as technical as it sounds.

Model the brain

The raw intelligence — the engine. Opus 4.8, Fable 5, GPT-5.5. This is the part that "thinks."

Harness the body

The arms and legs around the brain — what lets it actually do things. Claude, Codex, Hermes. You can drop almost any brain into any body.

Frontier vs. local big vs. free

Frontier = the biggest, smartest models (can't run on your laptop). Local/open-source = free, run on your own machine, less powerful, work offline.

Routing right tool, right job

Match the task to the model. Simple task → cheap, fast model. Hard task → smart model. Saves money and time.

Token the unit of cost

How AIs read and write. More tokens = more money + slower. "Token-hungry" = a process doing it the long, wasteful way.

MCP the Lego connector

The standard plug that lets your AI connect to apps — your messages, your calendar, an image generator. Snaps things together.

Skill a saved routine

A repeatable process you trigger by name. Runs the same way every time, so you stop re-explaining a task you do often.

Human in the loop the safety check

It checks with you before anything goes out — sending, posting, buying. You stay the one who hits send.

Cognitive debt the trap

The more you blindly outsource the decisions, the less you understand your own system — until you can't drive the thing you built.

Data sovereignty the whole point

Your context lives in your folder, not a company's servers. The librarian can change; the library stays yours.

The build

04The architecture, one layer at a time

Don't build six things at once — you'll drown. Build one, get annoyed at what it can't do yet, then build the next. Here's the whole stack, top (always loaded) to bottom (loaded only when needed).

1

Identity

You + AI write it · read every session

Who you are, who it is, the rules, where the truth lives. The move: don't write it from a blank page — have it interview you. "Ask me how I like to work, then I'll voice-dump." Start here.

2

Active context

You + AI write it · read every session, overwritten weekly

Where you are this week, what's loud, what's paused. It can even ping you on a schedule — you reply with a voice memo, it updates the file. This is your second layer.

3

Memory

AI writes it · loaded on demand

People in your world, results of past sessions, anything you flag with "remember this." It's a folder, not one file — it searches and pulls only what's relevant. Build this up over time.

4

Knowledge base (your wiki)

AI compiles it · loaded on demand

Your personal Wikipedia — everything you learn, distilled and cross-linked. Kept separate so it doesn't hog the AI's attention on every little request. (Projects live around here too — a folder per project so the context stays with the work.)

The one distinction people trip on: active context is the whiteboard at the front of the library — here's what's happening right now, wiped and rewritten weekly. Memory is all the books in the back — durable, only pulled when a task needs it. Same person can live in both: "prepping a client session Friday" goes on the whiteboard; "who this client is and what works for them" goes in the books.

Proof

05What people actually watched it do

The magic isn't any single task. It's that you never rebuild the scaffolding — it already knows you, so a lazy one-line prompt gets a real result.

◆ A whole website in ~20 minutes

One vague prompt: "build me a facilitation site." It went and read the working context, the brand voice, the pricing sheet, and even cropped photos already sitting in the folder. No one told it where to look — it just knew.

◆ Fact-checking a decision against your own profile

Hand it an article, ask "is this a good move for me?" It cross-references a profile you've already written, does the research, and turns the dense dump into a readable HTML with diagrams — "for the dumb-dumb brain," as I lovingly call mine.

◆ Marketplace on autopilot

Snap a photo of something you're selling. It writes the listing, runs a price comparison, generates extra product shots, posts it, triages the buyers, and pings you: "you've got three interested — who do you want?" (It always checks before it sends. Otherwise it'll cheerfully offer free shipping to Japan.)

◆ Taming the inbox

One app pulls every messaging platform into one place. The agent tells you who's actually waiting on a reply. You voice-note "tell them I'll be there at four," it drafts, you glance, you approve, it sends. Dictate-and-send — like having an assistant who already knows everyone.

The craft

06How to actually work with it

This is the stuff that took me months to learn and would've taken minutes to be told. So here it is.

A

Use your voice. Seriously, today.

Lowest-hanging fruit there is. A free, local tool (TypeWhisper) — hit a key, talk, it pastes anywhere. Talking gives the AI ten times the context typing does, because you stop self-editing. If you change one thing from this whole guide, change this.

B

Make it ask you questions

Don't say "make it better" and hope. Say "ask me questions about how to make it better" — then voice-dump your messy thoughts and let it sort them out. Socratic prompting. The AI is great at organizing your thinking once you actually give it your thinking.

C

Build the case study first, the skill second · Souki's method

The instinct is to build the template up front. Don't — that's like writing the recipe before you've cooked the dish. Do the full task once, by hand, with the AI. Get an output you genuinely love. Then point skill-creator at the whole conversation and say "turn this into a skill." Bonus: re-run the distilled version in a fresh chat to check it's not taking the scenic, token-hungry route.

D

Stop "prompt engineering." Add context instead.

Fancy prompt formatting is basically a bet that you're smarter than the model — and that bet gets worse every month. When you're not getting what you want, the fix is almost never a cleverer prompt. It's more context. Treat it like a sharp new assistant who just needs the backstory, not a magic incantation.

E

Keep a human in the loop

Anything that leaves your hands — a message, a post, a purchase — gets a check-in first. Build trust task by task. Automate the boring inputs; keep your thumb on anything that touches another human.

F

Watch your cognitive debt

"Just build it for me" is fine for a one-off output. For the system itself, it's a trap — delegate every decision and you end up with a machine you can't drive or fix. Understand the thing you're building, at least enough to steer it.

G

Stand on the shoulders of giants

Start from a template (mine, or anyone's) and tweak it to fit you — don't invent a hundred new buckets from scratch. It gets feral fast. Trim the fat regularly, and set up something that periodically cleans itself, or the whole thing bloats into a mess you don't recognize.

H

Build plateaus so you don't get lost in the sauce · Souki's method

It is genuinely easy to vanish into this for twelve hours and come out fried. Keep an open note: what am I trying to do, what have I done, what's next. Land there whenever you feel yourself floating. And celebrate the tiny wins — go smell something nice, do a jumping jack, look out a window. Give your brain a plateau to stand on between the leaps.

★

The meta-move: ask the model how to use itself

Stuck on literally anything? Type "how do I use you?" or "I don't know what I'm doing, walk me through it." It will. This one tool collapses most of the learning curve.

Go

07Your first 30 minutes

We're doing Claude, on your laptop. Not Hermes, not always-on, not the terminal wizard stuff — just the simplest version that works. You can climb later.

1

Make a folder

Put it in your home/user folder (not the desktop — it gets broader permissions there). Call it whatever you want. Drag it into your Finder sidebar so you never have to hunt for it.

2

Get the Claude desktop app

The desktop app, not the browser version. You'll want at least Pro (~$20/mo). You'll hit limits eventually — when you do, Max is genuinely worth it, it's a huge jump.

3

Point it at your folder

Open Cowork (simpler) or Code (more power — internet + file access). Select your folder, hit "trust." That folder is now its brain. The same move works for Codex or any other harness later.

4

Build your identity doc

Drop in a starter template, say "I want my own version — interview me about how I work," and voice-dump the answers. That's layer one, done. Active context comes next, memory builds over time.

5

Build smart while you're building the system

Use the smartest model at the highest effort for infrastructure work — Opus 4.8, not Haiku. You build the foundation once; build it sharp. Save the cheap, fast models for everyday small tasks.

6

Build one skill, the right way

Use the case-study-first method from above. A great first one is a wrap-up skill: at the end of any session, it files everything back into the right corners of your second brain automatically. It's the one I run most.

The map

08The three levels (don't skip)

Knowing the whole climb makes the first step less scary — and stops you from sprinting to level two and faceplanting.

Level 1 · start here

Local

The folder on your laptop, read by Claude. Simple, safe, owned by you. Live here for a while. Get comfortable. Get annoyed.

Level 2 · later

Always-on

Put a harness on a rented, always-on computer (~$8/mo), mirror the folder to the cloud, and talk to it from your phone — anywhere. Don't start here. Wait until you're annoyed you don't have it. Then build it.

Level 3 · the horizon

Fleets

A crew of agents with an orchestrator running cheaper sub-agents — a little org of AI employees, cheap interns plus senior models. Not now. Just know the ceiling is absurdly high.

Last thing

Let yourself be bad at it first

This is v1 and it shows — half the workshop was us fumbling through installing Git. That's kind of the point. This is a genuinely new skill, and the only way I learned it was by being bad at it for a while. Two months ago someone asked me if AI agents were useful yet and I said "honestly, no." I was wrong within weeks.

The wild part is the ceiling. Once it clicks, the only real limit is your imagination — annoying, but true.

Don't know where to start? Take this whole guide, hand it to your AI, and say: "I don't know what I'm doing — walk me through it step by step." That's not a cop-out. That's exactly how it's supposed to work.

Questions, breakthroughs, things that broke — bring them. We're all alpha-testing our own brains here.
